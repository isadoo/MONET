       \large A Method for Identifying Local Adaptation in Structured Populations}
\author{Isabela do O, Oscar E Gaggiotti, Pierre de Villemereuil, Jerome Goudet}
\date{\today}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Distinguishing adaptive divergence from neutral processes in structured populations is a fundamental challenge in evolutionary biology. Traditional methods like $Q_{ST}$-$F_{ST}$ comparisons assume equal relatedness among all subpopulations—an assumption that is often violated in natural metapopulations. When this assumption is broken, these methods can produce inflated false positive rates.

The LAVA package implements the LogAV method, which addresses this limitation by explicitly modeling population structure through coancestry matrices. The method compares two estimates of ancestral additive genetic variance derived from different levels of the demographic history. Under neutrality, these estimates should be equal; deviations indicate selection. LAVA maintains proper statistical calibration across diverse population structures while achieving comparable or superior power to existing methods.

This vignette provides a practical guide to using LAVA, from installation through analysis and interpretation. We demonstrate the workflow with a complete tutorial using simulated data, explain how to prepare your own data, and provide guidance on advanced usage and troubleshooting.
\end{abstract}

\section{Quick Overview}

\subsection{The LAVA Workflow}

LAVA follows a three-step workflow:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Calculate coancestries}: Use the \texttt{calculate\_coancestries()} function to compute population-level ($\mathbf{\Theta_P}$) and individual-level ($\mathbf{M}$) coancestry matrices from genetic data.

\item \textbf{Run the analysis}: Use the \texttt{lava()} function to fit a Bayesian mixed-effects model to your trait data, accounting for population structure.

\item \textbf{Interpret results}: Examine the log-ratio of ancestral variances and its associated p-value to assess evidence for local or global adaptation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Core Statistical Test}

LAVA tests for local adaptation by comparing two estimates of the ancestral additive genetic variance ($V_\mathcal{A}$):

\begin{itemize}
\item $\hat{V}_{A,B}$: estimated from between-population divergence
\item $\hat{V}_{A,W}$: estimated from within-population variation
\end{itemize}

Under neutrality, these two estimates should be equal on average: $E[V_{ \mathcal{A},B}] = E[V_{ \mathcal{A},W}]$, as both reflect the same ancestral variance partitioned through different aspects of the demographic history.

The test statistic is the log-ratio:
$$\text{LogAV} = \log\left(\frac{\hat{V}_{A,B}}{\hat{V}_{A,W}}\right)$$

Under neutrality, $E[\text{LogAV}] = 0$. 

\begin{itemize}
\item If LogAV is significantly $> 0$ (i.e., $V_{ \mathcal{A},B} > V_{ \mathcal{A},W}$): This indicates \textbf{local adaptation}. Phenotypic divergence among populations exceeds neutral expectations.

\item If LogAV is significantly $< 0$ (i.e., $V_{ \mathcal{A},B} < V_{ \mathcal{A},W}$): This indicates \textbf{global (or spatially-uniform) adaptation}. Selection has reduced divergence among populations relative to what would be expected under drift alone.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Statistical Inference}

LAVA uses Bayesian inference to compute the log-ratio. From the posterior distributions of $V_{ \mathcal{A},B}$ and $V_{ \mathcal{A},W}$, we calculate the log-ratio for each posterior sample. The Bayesian p-value is computed as:

$$p = 2 \times \min \left( \frac{\#(\text{LogAV} > \text{median})}{N}, \frac{\#(\text{LogAV} < \text{median})}{N} \right)$$

where $N$ is the number of posterior samples, this p-value quantifies the proportion of the posterior in which the log-ratio has the opposite sign to the median, effectively testing whether the log-ratio differs significantly from zero.

A small p-value (typically $p < 0.05$) provides evidence against neutrality and supports the presence of selection.

\subsection{Testing Environmental Correlations}
LAVA can also test whether trait values are linearly associated with environmental differences across populations. For that, environmental covariates should be included in the model (via the  \lstinline{formula_covariates} parameter). This test uses the coefficient $\beta$ from the fixed effects in the model.

For each environmental variable j, LAVA computes a Bayesian p-value:
\begin{equation}
    p_j = 2 \times \min\left(\frac{\#(\beta_j > 0)}{N}, \frac{\#(\beta_j < 0)}{N}\right)
\label{eq:pval_beta}
\end{equation}

This tests whether the environmental coefficient differs significantly from zero. A significant coefficient indicates that trait values systematically vary with that environmental variable across populations, even after accounting for neutral population structure through the random effects.

Both tests (LogAV and the environmental coefficient) can be significant simultaneously, providing both evidence for selection (LogAV) and insight into the ecological driver (environmental coefficient).

\section{Data Preparation}

\subsection{Data Requirements}

To use LAVA, you need:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Phenotypic data from a common garden}: Quantitative trait measurements from offspring (F1 generation) reared in a controlled environment to minimize phenotypic plasticity.

\item \textbf{Genetic markers from the parental (founder) generation}: Neutral markers (SNPs, microsatellites, etc.) from individuals sampled from wild populations. This is used to estimate the population-level coancestry matrix $\Theta_P$.

\item \textbf{Relatedness information for F1 individuals}: Either:
   \begin{itemize}
   \item Genetic data from F1 individuals (for estimating kinship), or
   \item Pedigree information (dam and sire IDs for each F1 individual)
   \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Population identification}: A mapping that links both parental and F1 individuals to their respective source populations.

\item \textbf{(Optional) Environmental data}: Environmental covariates measured at each source population can be included to test hypotheses about specific ecological drivers of divergence and to increase statistical power.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Data Format Requirements}

\subsubsection{Genetic Data (Dosage Format)}

Genetic marker data must be in \textbf{dosage format}, where each genotype is encoded as:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{0}: homozygous for the reference allele
\item \textbf{1}: heterozygous
\item \textbf{2}: homozygous for the alternative allele
\end{itemize}

The data should be organized as a matrix or data frame with:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Rows}: individuals
\item \textbf{Columns}: loci (genetic markers)
\end{itemize}

For example, if you have $n$ individuals genotyped at $L$ loci, your dosage matrix should have dimensions $n \times L$.

\textbf{Example structure}:
\begin{lstlisting}
head(genetic_data_parents[, 1:6])
#   locus1  locus2  locus3  locus4  locus5
#   1       0       2       1       0
#   0       1       1       2       1
#   2       2       0       0       2
\end{lstlisting}

Row names should be individual IDs, and column names should be locus IDs.

\subsubsection{Population Identification}

Population information for the parental generation should be provided as a \textbf{vector} of population IDs, with one entry per individual, in the same order as the rows in the genetic data:

\begin{lstlisting}
# Example for 6 individuals from 3 populations
parent_populations <- c("pop1", "pop1", "pop2", "pop2", "pop3", "pop3")
\end{lstlisting}

For the F1 generation, provide a \textbf{data frame} with at least two columns:
\begin{itemize}
\item One column for individual IDs
\item One column for population IDs
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
head(population_individual_id)
#   id      population_id
#   F001    pop1
#   F002    pop1  
#   F003    pop1
#   F004    pop2
\end{lstlisting}

\subsubsection{Phenotypic Data}

Trait data should be a data frame with at least two columns:
\begin{itemize}
\item One column for individual IDs (matching the F1 individuals)
\item One column for trait values
\end{itemize}

\begin{lstlisting}
head(trait_data)
#   id      trait_value
#   F001    15.2
#   F002    14.8
#   F003    16.1
#   F004    13.9
\end{lstlisting}

You can include additional columns for:
\begin{itemize}
\item Population IDs (if not provided separately)
\item Environmental covariates
\item Other fixed effects (e.g., sex, age, block effects)
\item Standard errors for trait measurements (if available)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Pedigree Data (if using instead of F1 genetic data)}

If you are using pedigree information instead of F1 genetic data, provide a data frame with at least these columns:
\begin{itemize}
\item Individual ID
\item Dam (mother) ID
\item Sire (father) ID
\item Dam population ID
\item Sire population ID
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Converting VCF to Dosage Format}

If your genetic data is in VCF (Variant Call Format), you need to convert it to dosage format. One way is through \textbf{hierfstat} read.VCF function:

\begin{lstlisting}
bed <- hierfstat::read.VCF
dos <- gaston::as.matrix(bed)
\end{lstlisting}

Some of the many other approaches include: (1) vcftools \cite{danecek2011variant} and R. (2) the package vcfR in R \cite{knaus2017vcfr}. (3) PLINK \cite{weeks2010plink} and R. 


\subsection{Reconstructing Parental Genotypes}

If you have genetic data only from the F1 generation and lack parental genetic data, you may be able to reconstruct parental genotypes using specialized software. This is particularly useful when you have pedigree information but did not genotype the parental generation. Some of these software include COLONY \cite{jones2010colony}, and Sequoia \cite{huisman2017pedigree}.

\textbf{Important considerations:}
Here are some considerations when using parental reconstructions, but we recommend reading the author's recommendations for each software.

\begin{itemize}
\item Parental genotype reconstruction is most reliable when you have:
  \begin{itemize}
  \item High marker density (many SNPs)
  \item Multiple offspring per parent
  \item Known pedigree structure
  \end{itemize}
\item Reconstruction quality varies by individual—some parents may be confidently reconstructed while others remain uncertain
\item When using reconstructed genotypes, be aware that reconstruction errors can affect coancestry estimates. 
\end{itemize}

\section{Environmental information}
Including environmental covariates in your analysis can substantially increase statistical power to detect selection while simultaneously providing insight into the ecological drivers of divergence. 

Traditional approaches to studying local adaptation follow a two-step process: First testing for patterns of non-neutral divergence (for example through $Q_{ST}$ -- $F_{ST}$), and then through a separate analysis, regress population trait means against environmental variables. However, this this approach does not leverage environmental information in the test for divergence, and the regression does not account fo the non-independence between subpopulations. 

LAVA uses a mixed-effect framework which allows to address both steps simultaneously. It does so by incorporating environmental covariates directly into the model which is accounting for the population structure. In our benchmarking we observed substantial power gains when including environmental information (See paper \textcolor{red}{[how do we refer to it?]}). Importantly, these power gains do not inflate false positive rates. 

When environmental covariates are included, LAVA provides two complementary pieces of information: LogAV test which describes potential evidence for selection and the environmental coefficients which describes the direction and strength of the association between trait and environmental variation.

\textbf{Understanding the model output:}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{sd(Intercept) for $\sim$pop}: This is the estimated standard deviation of the population random effect, which equals $\sqrt{V_{ \mathcal{A},B}}$. Here: 1.13, so $V_{ \mathcal{A},B} \approx 1.13^2 = 1.28$.

\item \textbf{sd(Intercept) for $\sim$ind}: This is the estimated standard deviation of the individual random effect, which equals $\sqrt{V_{ \mathcal{A},W}}$. Here: 0.39, so $V_{ \mathcal{A},W} \approx 0.39^2 = 0.15$.

\item \textbf{sigma}: Residual standard deviation (environmental/non-additive genetic variation)

\end{itemize}

\end{itemize}

\section{Advanced Usage}

\subsection{Including Additional Covariates}

Beyond environmental variables, you may want to include other covariates in your model to account for confounding factors or experimental design elements. These could be biological ones (sex, age, body size) or experimental design factors (common garden block, measurement year, rearing container).

\textbf{How to include covariates:}

Covariates should be columns in your trait data frame, which should be the same name you include in your covariates formula. You specify them using the \texttt{formula\_covariates} parameter:

\begin{lstlisting}
# Example: Including sex as a covariate
result_with_sex <- lava(
  Theta.P               = Theta.P,
  M                     = M,
  trait_dataframe       = trait_data,
  column_individual     = "id",
  column_trait          = "trait_value",
  column_population     = "population",
  formula_covariates    = "sex"
)

# Multiple covariates
result_multiple <- lava(
  Theta.P               = Theta.P,
  M                     = M,
  trait_dataframe       = trait_data,
  column_individual     = "id",
  column_trait          = "trait_value",
  formula_covariates    = "sex + age + block"
)
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Important notes:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Continuous covariates are automatically standardized (mean-centered and scaled by standard deviation) before fitting
\item Categorical covariates should be coded as factors in R
\item The model estimates fixed effects for each covariate while still accounting for population structure through the random effects
\item You can examine covariate effects in the model summary
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Custom Model Specifications}

LAVA uses \texttt{brms} for model fitting, which provides extensive flexibility for customization. You can pass additional arguments to \texttt{brms} through the \texttt{lava()} function.

By default, LAVA uses the default priors from \texttt{brms}. However, you may want to specify custom priors based on your system or prior knowledge, doing the following:

  trait_dataframe   = trait_data,

  column_trait      = "trait_value",
  prior             = my_priors  # passed to brms
)
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Theoretical Background}

\subsection{The Problem with $Q_{ST}$-$F_{ST}$}

The classical approach to detecting local adaptation in quantitative traits is the $Q_{ST}$-$F_{ST}$ comparison \citep{spitze1993population}. This method compares quantitative trait differentiation, measured through $Q_{ST}$, to the neutral expectation calculated from neutral markers ($F_{ST}$). Under neutrality, $Q_{ST}$ should equal $F_{ST}$ on average.

$Q_{ST}$ is defined as:
$$Q_{ST} = \frac{V_B}{V_B + 2V_W}$$

where $V_B$ is the additive genetic variance between populations and $V_W$ is the additive genetic variance within populations.

The issue we were focusing on addressing with lava lies on a critical assumption of  $Q_{ST}$-$F_{ST}$ methods. $Q_{ST}$-$F_{ST}$ methods assume that all subpopulations are equally related, that is, they follow an island model with uniform migration rates and equal effective population sizes. Under this assumption, $F_{ST}$ is a scalar that adequately describes population structure.

However, metapopulations are not likely to follow an island model. Instead, we expect metapopulations to have isolation by distance, potentially hierarchical structures with some subpopulations clustering into distinct lineages, variable effective sizes with some subpopulations having higher inbreeding than others, and so on.

When population structure deviates from the island model, $Q_{ST}$-$F_{ST}$ comparisons can produce inflated false positive rates \citep{de2022common,do2025method}. This occurs because the method incorrectly assumes equal relatedness between all population pairs when calculating the expected neutral divergence.

LAVA addresses the limitations of $Q_{ST}$-$F_{ST}$ by explicitly modeling population structure using coancestry matrices rather than relying on a single summary statistic.

The method is based on the premise that, under neutrality, we can estimate the ancestral additive genetic variance in two independent ways, and these estimates should agree.

Consider a metapopulation that descended from a common ancestral population. The ancestral population had some additive genetic variance $V_\mathcal{A}$. As populations diverged through drift and limited gene flow, this ancestral variance became partitioned into between-population and within-population components.

We can estimate $V_\mathcal{A}$ using:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Between-population information} ($\hat{V}_{A,B}$): How populations diverged from each other
\item \textbf{Within-population information} ($\hat{V}_{A,W}$): How individuals within populations are related
\end{enumerate}

Under neutrality: $E[V_ \mathcal{{A},B}] = E[V_ \mathcal{{A},W}] = V_\mathcal{A}$

Under selection:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Local adaptation}: $V_ \mathcal{{A},B} > V_ \mathcal{{A},W}$ (populations diverged more than expected)
\item \textbf{Global adaptation}: $V_{ \mathcal{A},B} < V_{ \mathcal{A},W}$ (populations converged due to similar selection)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The Statistical Model}

LAVA fits a Bayesian linear mixed-effects model (through \texttt{brms} to the phenotypic data:

$$z = \mu + X\beta + a_p + a_i + \epsilon$$

where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $z$ is the vector of phenotypic measurements (standardized)
\item $\mu$ is the overall mean
\item $X\beta$ represents fixed effects (environmental covariates, experimental factors, etc.)
\item $a_p$ is the population-level random effect
\item $a_i$ is the individual-level random effect
\item $\epsilon$ is residual error
\end{itemize}

The random effects are distributed as:
\begin{align}
a_p &\sim N(0, 2\Theta_p V_{ \mathcal{A},B}) \\
a_i &\sim N(0, M V_{ \mathcal{A},W})
\end{align}

The model simultaneously estimates $V_{ \mathcal{A},B}$ and $V_{ \mathcal{A},W}$, along with all other parameters, using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling.

\subsubsection{The Test Statistic}

The log-ratio of the two variance estimates provides our test statistic:

$$\text{LogAV} = \log\left(\frac{\hat{V}_{ \mathcal{A},B}}{\hat{V}_{ \mathcal{A},W}}\right)$$

The Bayesian framework provides a full posterior distribution for LogAV, from which we compute the p-value.

\subsubsection{Population-Level Coancestry ($\Theta_P$)}

$\Theta_P$ is an $r \times r$ matrix (where $r$ is the number of populations) that describes relatedness among populations.

Element $\Theta_{P,ij}$ represents the average probability of identity by descent (IBD) between a random allele from population $i$ and a random allele from population $j$, relative to the ancestral population.

\textbf{Calculation:} LAVA uses the allele-sharing method of moments \citep{weir2017unified,goudet2023allele}:

$$\hat{\Theta}_P = \frac{\bar{A}^{\mathcal{P}}_s - \min(\bar{A}^{\mathcal{P}}_s)}{1 - \min(\bar{A}^{\mathcal{P}}_s)}$$

where $\bar{A}^{\mathcal{P}}_s$ is a matrix of population-level allele-sharing statistics. Diagonal elements $\Theta_{P,ii}$ represent average coancestry within population $i$. Off-diagonal elements $\Theta_{P,ij}$ represent coancestry between populations $i$ and $j$. Higher values indicate closer relatedness (more recent common ancestry or more gene flow). The matrix captures the complete demographic history and structure.

In a stepping-stone model, like the one we used in our tutorial section, you would expect populations that are closer together to have higher coancestry values, which is what we show in Figure \ref{fig:thetap}.

\end{figure}


\subsubsection{Individual-Level Relatedness ($M$)}

$M$ is an $n \times n$ matrix (where $n$ is the number of F1 individuals) that describes genetic relationships among individuals within populations.

For common garden designs with within-population crosses, $M$ is block-diagonal, with each block corresponding to one population.

The within-population relatedness is adjusted to account for the population-level coancestry:

$$\hat{M}_x = \text{relatedness}_x \times (1 - \hat{\Theta}^P_x)$$

where $\hat{\Theta}^P_x$ is the diagonal element of $\hat{\Theta}_P$ for population $x$.

This adjustment is critical because it ensures that $M$ captures relatedness beyond what is already explained by population membership.

$M$ is estimated from either the F1 genetic data (using the same allele-sharing method) or the pedigree information (using standard kinship coefficients).

You can see the structure of M used in the example dataset of our tutorial in Figure \ref{fig:M}.

\subsection{Common Questions}

\subsubsection{Can I use the same genetic data to calculate both $M$ and $\Theta_P$?}

Ideally, no. $\Theta_P$ should describe the neutral structure of the source populations in the wild, while $M$ describes relatedness among F1 individuals in the common garden. Measuring both through F1 will not consider that you have added one new generation of drift from the breeding process.


\subsubsection{Is a common garden necessary?}

Yes, we strongly recommend using a common garden or another strategy to standardize environmental effects.  Like $Q_{ST}$-$F_{ST}$ methods, LAVA results without common gardens can be confounded by phenotypic plasticity (or environmental effects) and genotype-by-environment interactions.  Without environmental standardization, you cannot reliably distinguish genetic differences from environmental effects or plasticity.
